An allergic reaction is the body’s protective mechanism to guard against disease and infection. But an anaphylactic reaction is a rapid, severe allergic reaction that occurs when a person is exposed to an allergen. Soon after the agent enters the body, there is a high-dose release of a dozen chemical weapons including histamine which causes massive swelling in an attempt to “flush out ” the intruder.

Signs and Symptoms of Severe Allergic Reactions

• Swelling of face, lips, tongue and skin around the eyes and neck

• Breathing difficulties and wheezing “Lump in the throat”

• Flushed, itchy skin and skin rash (Hives)

• Stridor (high-pitched squeak or wheeze)

• Rapid, bounding heartbeat

• Feeling of impending doom

• Low blood pressure

First Aid for Severe Allergic Reactions

1 Have the person sit or lie down in a comfortable position


2 Locate patient-prescribed auto-injector


3 Administer epinephrine with auto-injector unit as per directions or local Protocols


4 Monitor the airway


5 Call 911

Auto-injector administration

1 Remove the safety cap.


2 Hold the unit firmly while keeping your hand away from the tip.


3 Find the outside of the person’s thigh.


4 From a distance of about 1 to 2 inches, firmly jab the tip into the outer thigh until the injector activates. You will hear a click


5 Hold the injector into place for 10 seconds.


6 After giving the injection, place auto-injector back into the container needle-first.


7 Massage the injected site.


8 Treat for shock.


9 Assess the breathing.

A person may have more than one injector in the event that multiple doses are required to control the reaction.


Dosage for adult: 0.3 mg of epinephrine

Dosage for child: 0.15 mg of epinephrine
